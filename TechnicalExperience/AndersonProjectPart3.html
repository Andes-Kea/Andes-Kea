def questions():
    print("-----------------Research Question #1-----------------")
    print("Do strategies have an overall affect on the cost of a fire?")
    print()
    dfStrat = dfFireMerge[dfFireMerge["Strategy"].str.contains("FSPC|MULT|PZP")]
    st.MeansTest(dfStrat, "Strategy", "Cost")
    print("The p-value is .06 which is greater than .05. We fail to reject the null hypothesis and are unable to determine whether a ")
    print("strategy has an affect on the cost of a wildland fire.")
    print()
    print("-----------------Research Question #2-----------------")
    print("Is there a correlation between the number of days a fire burns and it's cost per acre?")
    print()
    length_model = ols("Cost_per_Acre ~ Length_Days", data = dfFireMerge).fit()
    st.mult_reg(length_model, dfFireMerge["Cost_per_Acre"])
    print(length_model.summary())
    print("The r-squared value is .192% which means it is unable to predict the variation within the data. That means the length of a fire")
    print("cannot directly predict the cost per acre. It is 20% effective")
    print()
    print("-----------------Research Question #3-----------------")
    print("Does the destructiveness of certain causes of fire relate to the cost?")
    print()
    destructive_model = ols("Cost ~ Buildings_Destroyed + Cause", data = dfFireMerge).fit()
    st.mult_reg(destructive_model, dfFireMerge["Cost"])
    print(destructive_model.summary())
    print("The r-squared value .032% (an utter abysmal value for regression prediction) This means buildings destroyed are not correlated")
    print("to the overall cost of a fire when comparing causes.")

def regression():
    print("Regression:")
    cos_per_acre_model = ols("Cost_per_Acre ~ Cost + Strategy + Acres", data = dfFireMerge).fit()
    st.mult_reg(cos_per_acre_model, dfFireMerge["Cost_per_Acre"])
    print(cos_per_acre_model.summary())

def graph():
    print("Graphing:")
    graphCause = dfFireMerge["Cost_per_Acre"].groupby(dfFireMerge["Cause"]).mean()
    graph = graphCause.plot.bar()
    graph.set_xlabel("Cause of Fire (Human, Lightning, Uknown)")
    graph.set_ylabel("Cost per Acre")
    graph.set_title("Cost per Acre vs Cause")
    plt.show()

Conclusion¶

Overall, when conducting research and analysis on my three research questions, I came to the conclusion that the size and cost of the fire (cost per acreage) is mainly determined by cause and length. However, due to the inherent nature that fire, and well, nature for that matter is unpredictable; attempting to predict the cost and size is fleeting. The three questions I asked in order to come to this overall conclusion were:

        1. What strategy affects the overall cost of a fire?
        2. Is there a correlation between the number of days a fire burns and it's cost per acre?
        3. Does the destructiveness of certain causes of wildland fire relate to the cost?

These questions helped narrow down the process of identifying certain key strategies, cuases, and other variables that may have a large impact in predicting / analyzing wildland fire behavior. For the strategy that that affects the overall cost of a fire, the anova test returned a p-value greater than 5% which means we fail to reject the null and unable to determine if a certain strategy has an affect on the cost of a fire. There was also an inconclusive conclusion to the second question. When analyzing the r-squared value and the prediction, there did not seem to be a correlation between the number of days a fire burns and it's cos per acre. This was surprising when looking at a real world application of the cost of a fire. I would have predicted that there would be a direct relationship between the length and cost of a fire. The third question also had an inconclusive result, or rather had an incredibly conclusive result. The r-squared value rests at .032%... absolutely abysmal. This means the model predicts essentially 0% of the variability within the data. A better predictor is using cost strategy and acres to predict the cost per acre. Though this is obvious, it still shows a correlation between the cost and size of a fire leading the to cost/acre.
